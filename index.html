Overview

What is ketosis? Ketosis is a metabolic state where your body burns fat as its primary fuel instead of carbohydrates. When you restrict carbs below ~20g net per day, your liver converts fatty acids into ketones, which power your brain and muscles. This state makes stored body fat far more accessible as an energy source.

How keto burns fat. Without carbs, insulin stays low. Low insulin unlocks your fat cells, allowing fatty acids to flow into the bloodstream and liver. Your body becomes a fat-burning machine — drawing on both food fat and stored body fat throughout the day.

Keto & Lipedema PMC Study

What is lipedema? Lipedema is a chronic condition involving abnormal fat deposits — typically in the hips, thighs, and legs — that is resistant to standard dieting. It affects predominantly women and is often mistaken for regular obesity.

Why keto helps. A 2023 study (PMC10748777) found that ketogenic diets significantly reduced lipedema-related fat volume, pain, and inflammation in participants. Unlike calorie restriction, which often fails with lipedema, ketosis appears to target the inflammatory, hormone-sensitive fat characteristic of the condition.

Key findings from the study:

Reduced fat volume
Measurable reduction in lipedema fat deposits over 8–12 weeks of strict keto
Lower inflammation
Ketones have direct anti-inflammatory effects, reducing the underlying driver of lipedema fat
Less pain & sensitivity
Participants reported reduced tenderness and improved quality of life

Research & Timelines

1–3

Days 1–3: Carb depletion

Glycogen stores deplete. You may feel tired, foggy, or irritable — the "keto flu." Water and electrolytes drop significantly. Drink more, add salt.

1w

Week 1: Ketosis begins

Blood ketones rise above 0.5 mmol/L. Energy stabilises. Rapid weight drop is mostly water loss — expected and healthy.

2–4

Weeks 2–4: Fat adaptation starts

Your muscles and brain begin preferring ketones. Mental clarity often improves. Appetite typically drops — this is normal and beneficial.

6–8

Weeks 6–8: Body recomposition

Measurable body fat loss visible. Inflammation markers drop. Energy is stable throughout the day without peaks and crashes.

3m

3+ months: Deep fat adaptation

Full fat adaptation achieved. Athletic performance returns or improves. Lipedema-specific fat begins responding if present.

Note: Track your measurements and photos — the scale doesn't capture everything. Fat loss and muscle gain can happen simultaneously, making weight a poor proxy for progress.

Myths vs. Evidence

Myth

Eating fat makes you fat

Evidence

Dietary fat without carbs doesn't spike insulin — the main fat-storage hormone

Myth

Keto destroys your kidneys

Evidence

No evidence for healthy individuals. Higher protein helps preserve muscle during fat loss

Myth

You need carbs for energy

Evidence

The brain can run on ketones. Adapted keto athletes perform comparably in most sports

Myth

Cholesterol will spike dangerously

Evidence

HDL typically rises; triglycerides drop. LDL response varies — monitor with your doctor
